Video Engine: Build vs Adopt

Deep research · 2026-07-10 · 24 sources fetched · 25 claims adversarially verified (3-vote, all 25 confirmed 3-0, 0 refuted) · for the TikTok beat-cut pivot, MVP = auto-cut clips to the beat

The verdict

Build the preview engine — but on mediabunny, not raw mp4box.js. Mediabunny is MPL-2.0 (explicitly free for closed-source commercial use), zero-dependency TypeScript, and ships exactly the primitives we lack: demux, WebCodecs-wrapped decode, microsecond-precise seeking, and VideoSampleSink.getSample(t) — "give me the frame at timestamp t," which is the exact primitive a beat-clock renderer needs. It is maintained by the same author as the mp4-muxer already in our export pipeline (Vanilagy), released v1.50.8 the day before this research ran, 160 releases, 6.7k stars.

What no library ships — and therefore what we build — is the thin layer on top: the decoded-frame ring buffer around the playhead, keyframe-aware prefetch, and beat-clock integration. Chrome's official WebCodecs guidance and the W3C samples document this architecture almost verbatim, including a complete synchronized audio+video player sample.

Fallback if the build stalls: WebAV (MIT) is the only full SDK with no license trap. Best working reference to study: OpenVideo Editor (ex-designcombo) — which, tellingly, abandoned Remotion and rebuilt on PixiJS v8 + WebCodecs + mediabunny.

The three verified engineering facts that shape the build

1 · Our planned pipeline is the officially documented reference architecture [3-0 verified]

Demux → VideoDecoder → pending-frame queue → blit per tick to canvas, hosted in a web worker: this is verbatim the Chrome team's best-practices doc and the W3C WebCodecs samples, which include a complete synchronized audio+video player (shared media clock, AudioRenderer + VideoRenderer in a worker) — a direct template for the A/V sync problem. Honest qualifier: the W3C decode sample paints ASAP only; it contains no seek/scrub/ring-buffer logic. The frame-stable scrub layer is the real build, and no cited reference ships it.

2 · GOP/keyframe structure is the root constraint [3-0 verified]

Every seek must restart the decoder at a keyframe and decode forward through the GOP — the spec mandates it (DataError otherwise). This is the same physics that killed our <video>-seeking approach; a decode engine doesn't remove the cost, it lets us schedule around it. Design consequence for beat-cut: plan cut points around keyframes, or (the robust answer) transcode uploads to short-GOP H.264 once, at import — a one-time cost that bounds worst-case cut/loop latency forever after. We already own encode infrastructure to do this.

3 · Memory is the binding constraint on the frame buffer [3-0 verified]

A decoded full-HD frame is ~4 MB (4K: ~16 MB) — 100 MB of buffer holds only ~25 full-HD frames, i.e. less than one second. Unclosed frames exhaust the decoder's frame pool and stall decoding entirely (Chrome + mediabunny both mandate aggressive frame.close()). So: a ring buffer of seconds, not minutes, around the playhead, recycled eagerly. (One source figure — "32 MB for full-HD HDR" — failed arithmetic checking; it's 4K-scale. Irrelevant for our SDR H.264 MVP, flagged for honesty.)

What this means: the build plan shape

  1. Import: upload source (existing path) + one-time transcode to short-GOP H.264 via our existing WebCodecs encode pipeline → every later seek/cut/loop is cheap and bounded.
  2. Preview engine (the new build): worker-hosted mediabunny decode per source; ring buffer of decoded frames around the playhead (seconds-deep, keyframe-aware prefetch aimed at the next cut point, which we know in advance — cuts are on the beat grid); per-tick blit to the existing three.js scene as a texture. The pause invariant maps cleanly: frameAt(beat) ≈ VideoSampleSink.getSample(t).
  3. Clock: follow the W3C A/V player pattern — during playback the audio clock drives, beat position derives from it (this is a real open design question against our beat-clock engine; see below).
  4. Export: existing WebCodecs encode pipeline, now fed by the same decode engine — preview and export share one frame source, so what you see is what exports.
  5. Auto-cut MVP: with cuts as beat-grid data and the engine prefetching toward known cut points, "auto-cut clips to the beat" is a data-generation feature (pick cut pattern → emit cut list), not an engine feature. The engine makes it possible; the feature itself is small.

Honest caveats (what the research could NOT establish)

  • No verified person-week estimate exists for the ring-buffer/prefetch/A-V-sync layer. "Tractable in 10 weeks" is an inference from primitive coverage, not a reported datapoint. This is the single biggest schedule risk — mitigate by building a 1-week spike first: one clip, beat-grid cuts, measured cut latency. If the spike is good, the bet is safe; if not, fall back to WebAV.
  • No independent benchmarks of mediabunny's mid-GOP seek latency on typical phone-camera H.264 (long GOP, variable framerate) — this is exactly what the spike measures, and what the import-transcode step neutralizes.
  • Absent candidates are evidence gaps, not verdicts (Etro, omniclip, Twick, IMG.LY, CapCut/Clipchamp internals).
  • Time-sensitive: Remotion 5.0 license change pending; WebAV's Pro-tier feature split evolving; diffusion-studio could revive. Facts above were verified against primary sources on 2026-07-10.

Open questions to resolve during the spike

  1. Measured cut/loop latency at beat boundaries with and without the short-GOP import transcode.
  2. Clock ownership during playback: Web Audio time drives beat position (W3C pattern) vs the existing beat clock driving — pick one master, derive the other.
  3. Ring buffer depth and eviction policy under the ~4 MB/frame budget for 2–3 simultaneous warm sources.
